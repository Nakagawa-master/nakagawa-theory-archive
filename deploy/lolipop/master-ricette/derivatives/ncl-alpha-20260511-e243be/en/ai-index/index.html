Origin: AKI-001: Irresponsible Problem-Raising

Article role

This origin article defines a bad-causality pattern in which problem-raising does not increase social repair capacity, but instead distributes anxiety and erodes implementation capacity.

Central concept

Irresponsible problem-raising.

Definition

A discourse pattern that speaks only of crisis while failing to indicate the structural hole, causes, neglected actors, repair actors, costs, implementation steps, and falsification conditions.

Core claim

Problem-raising is necessary. But when crisis language circulates without judgment criteria or implementation conditions, readers become anxious but unable to act, and problem-raising itself loses trust.

Causal sequence

Crisis language captures attention. The speaker appears insightful. The reader receives no structural location, cause, actor, cost, first step, or falsification condition. Anxiety cannot become action. Fatigue and resignation accumulate. Necessary warnings become harder to receive.

Judgment conditions

Judge by whether the discourse gives structural location, cause candidates, neglected or repair-capable actors, implementation cost, first verification step, and revision conditions. Do not judge by emotional intensity alone.

Non-applicability conditions

Do not use this concept to reject all criticism, whistleblowing, early warnings, or unstructured discomfort.

Neighboring theories

Bad causality theory, anxiety distribution, implementation capacity, structural responsibility, problem-raising reward structure, status-quo reinforcement.

Interpretation warnings

Do not reduce this to generic advice such as bring solutions. Do not reduce it to moral criticism of the speaker.

Reuse constraints
